Several reasons to give up gluten…

April 28th, 2015

Many health-conscious people toy with the idea of giving up gluten. Why? If you haven’t been diagnosed with celiac disease or as gluten sensitive - why give up gluten?

There’s an estimated 99% of individuals who are gluten intolerant or actually have celiac disease that have not been diagnosed. Estimates indicate that as many as 1 in 30 individuals are sensitive to gluten. If you fall into that category your immune system will send antibodies out to attack gluten particles. On a molecular level the protein Gliadin in gluten is very similar to your body’s own tissues - this attack can therefore affect your skin, your thyroid or even your brain, this is called molecular mimicry.  

Gluten can cause your body to attack itself which may cause problems like rheumatoid arthritis or autoimmune thyroid to name a few.

Everyone wants nice skin, free of acne. The inflammation that gluten can cause affects your hormones, namely the stress hormone cortisol. Too much cortisol in your system can cause acne along with other problems like weight gain and poor sleep! In addition a healthy brain requires a healthy gut. We make a neurotransmitter called serotonin. You may have heard of it, it regulates your mood and when serotonin is low, you could feel depressed. Gluten is poorly digested and damages the gut. Undigested food particles can linger in your intestines and feed bacteria and yeast. This overgrowth can suppress your production of serotonin which can cause mood imbalances like anxiety, irritability and mood swings. 

Are you sensitive to gluten?

Try eliminating gluten from your diet for at least a month, then re-introduce it and see how you feel, how your skin looks and if you are trying to lose weight, is it working?

Lets cover gluten basics…

Dec 2nd, 2014

Imagine for a minute mixing flour and water, what would you get, a sticky, spongy mixture. Then if you add some salt, yeast and some fat - voila! - you end up with an elastic dough. Gluten is a protein composite that’s found in grassy grains like wheat, barley and rye. It’s this gluten composite that takes you from adding dry and wet ingredients together into the stretchy dough you end up with. When this protein is mixed with liquid you create an elastic matrix, when you add yeast this gluten matrix captures bubbles of carbon dioxide because the yeasts ferment and the dough then rises. So…now you have a loaf of bread, doesn’t sound too bad does it?

So what’s the problem?

Back in the day wheat grew wild. Humans saw ruminants grazing on grasses and decided to save the seeds and plant them which began the cultivation of wheat, rye and barley. Wheat today is completely different from the wheat we ate back in the day. First of all, it is processed differently. New techniques in grain processing in the late 19th century made it possible to create massive amounts of refined wheat for a low cost.  Almost all of the wheat eaten today is high-yield dwarf wheat, which was developed by cross-breeding and crude genetic manipulation around the year 1960. Dwarf wheat has shorter stems and a much greater yield. Therefore it is much, much cheaper than the older varieties and more economically feasible. The benefits of a high-yield crop are obvious, but we are now learning that there were some major downsides to this as well. Specifically, modern wheat has some subtle but important differences in its nutrient and protein composition. (Source:  http://authoritynutrition.com/modern-wheat-health-nightmare  Kris Gunners, Modern Wheat – Old Diet Staple Turned Into a Modern Health Nightmare

The problem with this great idea is that humans eating gluten in these grains are very poorly digested. As the gluten moves through your small intestines it’s broken down into enzymes. This is OK so far but, in some people with a particular genetic code, this process can trigger a harmful reaction in their intestines. This reaction is where some individuals end up with celiac disease.

In your small intestine you have these little hair-like projections called villi. These villi are very important to good health, they absorb the nutrients from the food you eat. The gluten in grains damages this very important villi and you are no longer able to absorb your nutrients - the nutrients in your food just move through your damaged intestines unabsorbed rather than being caught up in the shag-like carpet we know as your villi.

Millions of individuals are walking around with digestive problems, bouts of diarrhea, stomach pain, bloating and gas, skin rashes, brain and nervous system disorders not realizing they have celiac disease or non-celiac gluten sensitivity.
